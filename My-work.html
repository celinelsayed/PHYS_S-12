Lesson 2: 2D Design & Cutting

Assignment: Document our learning as we work through a Fusion360 tutorial. Then select 2 components from the kit or random household objects, and model them in Fusion360.

Seeing as I'd only used CAD software once in my life three years ago, i knew this assignment would be a challenge. It had also been my firt time using calipers so everything seemeed quite foreign. However, after watching numerous tutorials and getting to mess around with the software, I now feel confident in my ability to use Fusion360 to create sketches and 3D Models.

My first step was to work through this tutorial and gain an understanding of the basics. While it was mainly a refresher of what we had learned in class using the sketch, extrude, offset, etc. features, it was still very concise and ensured that I had a sound understanding of those features before i began. 

For this weeks assignment, I decided to model a binder clip (mainly becuase i had one infront of me) and an LED light strip from the kit we recieved. Here are the parts I set out to model. 

Firstly, I made the Binder Clip. I Started with measuring its dimensons with the caliper (as you can see below), and split it into two main components. While im not sure of the specific name, I seperated it into the metal loop, and clip itself. I started off with the clip. I realized the easiest way would be to split the clip in half and then simply mirror the design. I realized the metal of the clip was not straight, but instead had a curviture to do it, however i knew this would be difficult to do with the calipers so i ended up estimating the values for the radius. I wanted to make this as accurate as possible, so i ensured I also made the area where the loop component would fit in. I then used the offset tool to create a 'useable' binder clip, rather than having a prism block.

After finishing the sketch, the rest was relatively simple. I extruded the sketch to a thickness of 0.25mm, and completed the final details, such as using the trim tool to cut out the pinch at the front. Finally i then mirrored the body in the x-axis, now finally having my full component of the clip.  I followed a similair technique with the loop. I made a sketch using the line, spline and trim tools, then used the pipe option to bring it into 3D, then finally mirrored it. 

The final step was the assembly. I had on loop and needed two so I made a copy of the loop component. Now I had the three components, but they were independent of one another. So I then used the assemble tool to line up the edge of the loop with the hollow cave i'd made previously. This proved to be more difficult then I expected as I couldn't get them to line up properly at first. The metal loop seemed to be at a tangent to the clip. Finally i got it to work, and adjusted the angle between the loop and clip to make it look more realistic. I repeated this on the other side.

Then I made a circuit using a potentiometer, which works by varying the position of a sliding contact across a uniform resistance. Rapid Protoyping: Kinetic Sculpture

Creating a moving replica of Leonardo Da Vinci's flying machine as a kinetic scultpture.

Lesson 4: Rapid Prototyping

Assignment: Make and document either (a) a kinetic sculpture or (b) a quick prototype of a possible final project. This should integrate an electronic circuit with physical components. In addition to components in the kit, feel free to use materials you find around the house (cardboard, toothpicks, coat hangers, tape and glue, etc.).

For my kinetic sculpture, I decided to create a replica of Leonardo Da Vinci's flying machine as a kinetic sculpture. I took some inspiration from
Michelle Luh's Dragon, and my initial plan was to closely follow her documentation.


Aviation was one of Leonardo Da Vinci's favorite areas of study, and he was extremly excited about the idea of people roaming the sky like birds, leading to the invention of the 'Flying Machine' - arguably one of his most known inventions - which conveyed his imagination, enthusiasm and love for flying. However, he never managed to build the actual device (even if it was unlikely to be succesful). Leonardo da Vinci's work has always intrigued me from his most famous work (such as the Mona Lisa and The Last Supper painting) to The armored car (another mild obsession), so I really wanted to try and create a memento of his work, which I could keep for myself.


My first step was to create designs of the sculpture. I decided to use the app AdobeDraw on my iPad to digitally design these, so I had a better understanding of what to do. I got my basic shape from this illustration, and since it didnt show the entire device, I estimated how the rest would look. Using Adobe Draw and quite a bit of time I managed to illustrate the plan at the bottom, showing the entire design of the flying machine. I then made a simple 3D Geometric design of the center as I knew I couldn't create a center with such complexity.
 

I then started to create the device itself, I used wooden dowels to create the center, following the geometric design I already made. I attempted to stick to two lengths, so it wouldn't be too difficult, full size and half. Splitting the dowels proved to be alot harder than i expected, as I didn't have the proper tools at home to split them, instead I had to carve out a substantial amount, and then snap the dowel where the carve had weakened them. While this method worked 15 out of 16 times, it meant I ended up with alot of splinters. Next time I'll definitely be sure to have the proper tools to split them and/or the right safety precautions (gloves). Obviously my half dowels were not all the exact same length, so i attempted to pair them together according to length so they'd be more effective in use, i.e. I used the longer dowels for the x dimension, and the shorter ones for the y, this way there was great difference. I used hot glue to stick all the dowel pieces together, adding extra ones in the middle for support. I then used the dowels in this structure to establish the scale for the rest of my device, using my previous sketch. This meant i then had all the dimensions for the plane, and transferred it to construction paper. As seen in the photo above, I then was able to plot the exact points of the wings and tail, and easily draw the basic outline of the pieces.

Following Michelle's page, my first task was to build a camshaft and followers to be able to convert the rotary motion of the motor into linear. I used youtube to help me find a tutorial on how to achieve this, but most of them were on too large of a scale, so instead of being able to create multiple followers, it would instead just be one. I realized this process would be more one of trial and error and used this video to help improve my understanding, and help me decide what kind of motion i would like. I decided to follow the eccentric cam, and use that, as i wanted a smoother movement, and didn't need it to go too high. In the video he was able to cut and create his own cams from wood, but I knew i didn't have the resources for that, so instead I drew inspiration from his cardboard tutorial at the end. As I didn't have enough plastic bottle caps at home (#stopusingplastic), I instead had to make my own using whatever cardboard I could find (which in my case was my sister's balloon blower box). I cut out 12 circles with a radius of 1.5 cm, and quickly drank 6 bottles of water, so I could use its bottle caps. I hotglued a bottle cap between each 2 circles, and then cut out a strip of paper and glued that round to get a smooth edge. I then drilled a holl through it.  I repeated this with the other 5 caps to create my cams. 


Then came the assembly of the support system. Firstly I stuck together two the long skewer sticks together so I could control all 6 cams with one motor later, then using the cardboard from various boxes, I used the entire length of the cardboard, which meant I had to reinforce the creases using sticks to ensure it held up. I also used dowels to keep the two layers apart. I then poked holes throughout for my cams, however I realized it needed additional support to keep it from moving, so I had to add the middle layer to hold the sticks. The most difficult step of this part was cutting through the cardboard, as I only had scissors, meaning the simplest steps took longer and more effort than needed. 
(P.S ignore the messy room in the back, this step was one continous train of thought and didn't leave time to clean up inbetween) 
Then, I added my actual flying machine at the top, I hotglued the center, and used wooden sticks to create points where the wings could meet with the dowels without ripping. 

Then came the electronic componenet of my work. I wasn't too worried about this step as I already knew how I planned on automating my device, as I'd already attempted the circuit during class. I used the tutorial on how to use the  L9110 H Bridge Power Driver to drive a motor  using a potentiometer to control the speed of the motor as I knew I wanted it to be relatively slow. I combined the circuit with the Arduino code we were given on the class site (both of which can be seen below).

 Looking through Michelle's site I was able to anticipate some issues that could come up, so I worked to resolve them before I turned on the circuit, such as attaching the motor to wooden dowel at the camshaft, and the turning Metro board, motor driver and driver, so I attached both of those correctly, using hot glue and 3M tape (each where applicable). 

While it didn't work flawlessly, and some of the cam's would get stuck and need to be repositioned, the flying machine worked nonetheless (at least better than its original design would) . I was very happy with the outcome, and for the next time I choose to create a kinetic sculpture, I will learn from my mistakes, such as having the correct tools, and just in general being more careful (hot glue can be lethal), and use my new knowledge of manufacturing.

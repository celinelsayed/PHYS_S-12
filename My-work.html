2D Design & Cutting

Assignment: Document our learning as we work through a Fusion360 tutorial. Then select 2 components from the kit or random household objects, and model them in Fusion360.

Seeing as I'd only used CAD software once in my life three years ago, i knew this assignment would be a challenge. It had also been my firt time using calipers so everything seemeed quite foreign. However, after watching numerous tutorials and getting to mess around with the software, I now feel confident in my ability to use Fusion360 to create sketches and 3D Models.

My first step was to work through this tutorial and gain an understanding of the basics. While it was mainly a refresher of what we had learned in class using the sketch, extrude, offset, etc. features, it was still very concise and ensured that I had a sound understanding of those features before i began. 

For this weeks assignment, I decided to model a binder clip (mainly becuase i had one infront of me) and an LED light strip from the kit we recieved. Here are the parts I set out to model. 

Firstly, I made the Binder Clip. I Started with measuring its dimensons with the caliper (as you can see below), and split it into two main components. While im not sure of the specific name, I seperated it into the metal loop, and clip itself. I started off with the clip. I realized the easiest way would be to split the clip in half and then simply mirror the design. I realized the metal of the clip was not straight, but instead had a curviture to do it, however i knew this would be difficult to do with the calipers so i ended up estimating the values for the radius. I wanted to make this as accurate as possible, so i ensured I also made the area where the loop component would fit in. I then used the offset tool to create a 'useable' binder clip, rather than having a prism block.

After finishing the sketch, the rest was relatively simple. I extruded the sketch to a thickness of 0.25mm, and completed the final details, such as using the trim tool to cut out the pinch at the front. Finally i then mirrored the body in the x-axis, now finally having my full component of the clip.  I followed a similair technique with the loop. I made a sketch using the line, spline and trim tools, then used the pipe option to bring it into 3D, then finally mirrored it. 

The final step was the assembly. I had on loop and needed two so I made a copy of the loop component. Now I had the three components, but they were independent of one another. So I then used the assemble tool to line up the edge of the loop with the hollow cave i'd made previously. This proved to be more difficult then I expected as I couldn't get them to line up properly at first. The metal loop seemed to be at a tangent to the clip. Finally i got it to work, and adjusted the angle between the loop and clip to make it look more realistic. I repeated this on the other side.

My other part was the led strip. My biggest issue with the LED strip wasnt the actual construction of it, but instead due to the fine details of it and its small nature, it was extremely hard to measure using the caliper. I spent the longest time just getting the design of this LED strip down. Firstly i sketched the basic outline, and then started creating construction lines to aid my building of the many, many, parts to it later. Since the LED Strip is a series of repeating units, I decided to only create the first unit, then copy and paste them it. I then used the rectangle, spline and trim tool to construct a sketch of my first repeating unit, and adopting the trim tool to clean it up. I then repeated the pattern throughtout, to have a full sketch of the LED strip. 

I then began to extrude the different parts. As the LED strip was extremely skinny, recording measurements with the caliper and taking a photo of it proved to be impossible. Instead i just made quick notes of it as i worked on. I extruded each one to their corresponding height, then started working on its appearence, and selecting the correct material to suit each part (e.g. copper connecting pads, metal supporting circuitry, or the LED SMD Chip).
